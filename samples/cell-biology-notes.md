## Learning goals

- Distinguish passive transport from active transport.
- Explain why phospholipids form a bilayer in water.
- Predict the direction of osmosis in simple examples.

## Membrane structure

Phospholipids are amphipathic: each has a hydrophilic phosphate-containing head and hydrophobic fatty-acid tails. In water, they spontaneously arrange into a bilayer with heads facing aqueous environments and tails sheltered inside. The notes call the membrane “fully rigid” in one margin annotation, but the lecture summary describes it as a fluid mosaic in which many lipids and proteins move laterally.

Cholesterol sits among phospholipids in animal-cell membranes. At ordinary classroom temperatures, it helps moderate membrane fluidity. Integral proteins are embedded in the bilayer; peripheral proteins associate more loosely with a membrane surface.

## Transport

Passive transport does not directly require cellular energy and moves a substance down its concentration or electrochemical gradient. Simple diffusion crosses the lipid bilayer without a transport protein. Facilitated diffusion also moves down a gradient but uses a channel or carrier protein.

Active transport moves a substance against its gradient and requires an energy source. Primary active transport directly couples transport to an energy-releasing reaction such as ATP hydrolysis. Secondary active transport uses the energy stored in another ion's gradient.

## Osmosis

Osmosis is the net movement of water across a selectively permeable membrane toward the side with higher effective solute concentration. A cell placed in a hypotonic solution receives net water movement; a cell in a hypertonic solution loses net water. The notes do not cover how cell walls change the outcome.

## Example

A membrane separates side A, containing 1% nonpenetrating solute, from side B, containing 4% nonpenetrating solute. If only water crosses, net water movement is from A toward B until equilibrium is approached.
